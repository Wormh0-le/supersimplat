# Reconcile gsplat validity-boundary contributor flips against the RGB rasterization

The locked gsplat revision evaluates the shared per-Gaussian alpha in two separate CUDA translation units: the RGB rasterization kernel and the complete contributor-ID kernels. Their compiled float32 arithmetic disagrees by a few ulps, which is immaterial except exactly at the kernels' shared decision boundaries — the `1/255` validity cut, the `1e-4` transmittance termination cut, and sigma zero — where one kernel can accept a contributor the other rejected. Measured on the frozen controlled-overlap fixture at the locked 1008-pixel Anchor, exactly one of 1,016,064 pixels differed: the contributor stream accepted a Gaussian whose exact alpha is `0.003921566848368366`, `1.78e-9` below the validity cut, while the RGB kernel excluded it, producing a `0.0012227` mass mismatch (`rendererMassMismatch`) that blocked the production trial. The contributor kernels, not the RGB kernel, made the boundary error; the float64 reference chain reproduces the RGB alpha to `3.3e-8`.

The production renderer reconciles only proven boundary flips and fails closed on anything else. When a pixel's contributor mass exceeds the unchanged `2e-6 + 1e-5 * abs(alpha)` tolerance, the backend replays that pixel's tile from the same projection/tile preparation. The reference follows the source expression grouping with an explicit binary32 rounding after every scalar add, subtract, and multiply; alpha clamping and transmittance are also binary32. Python cannot invoke the locked CUDA translation unit's `__expf` intrinsic or reproduce its compiler contraction bit for bit, so it uses a correctly rounded binary32 exponential only to enumerate conservative candidate decisions. An intrinsic-specific disagreement that lacks a unique boundary explanation therefore fails closed rather than being treated as an exact replay.

A contributor is ambiguous only when the high-precision diagnostic evaluation sits within float32 evaluation noise of a boundary — `1e-8` of the validity cut (about 21 float32 ulps; the measured flip was `1.78e-9`, and `__expf`'s documented worst case at the cut's sigma of ~5.5 is ~8 ulps, plus float32 sigma-evaluation noise), `1e-9` of the termination cut, or `1e-9` of sigma zero — and at most four ambiguous contributors per pixel are enumerated. A genuine flip beyond the validity window fails closed rather than widening the class of defects the reconciler can mask. The repair is confined to a proven flip on an otherwise sound stream: the kernel stream's weights must be finite and must already reproduce the contributor kernels' own alpha within mass tolerance; exactly one replayed decision variant must reproduce the RGB alpha within `1e-6` and differ from exactly one replayed kernel-ID variant; and every weight in that kernel decision chain must agree with the kernel output within `max(1e-8, 16 float32 ulps)`. Checking the complete chain, including its post-flip tail, rejects balanced or otherwise hidden contributor-weight defects while still allowing ordinary locked-kernel evaluation noise. At the termination cut, the reconciler only models the source-faithful direction where the reference stays above the cut and the contributor kernel excludes the Gaussian; it never invents an inclusion when the replayed `next_T` is at or below the cut, because the alternate kernel's above-cut transmittance is unavailable. The pixel's contributor IDs and weights are then rebuilt from the RGB-matching chain, so attribution stays tied to the same rasterization that produced service RGB and alpha. The sealed Render Policy records `gsplat-boundary-contributor-reconciliation/v2`, an explicit reconciliation-policy revision; the editor RGB render configuration stays unchanged because this revises only the Companion's fail-closed proof, not the declared appearance semantics. Any mismatch no boundary variant explains — a missing or extra non-boundary contributor, a divergent raster alpha, a non-finite or inconsistent kernel weight, any material chain-weight defect, an unflipped kernel stream, more than four candidates, or multiple matching variants — still raises `rendererMassMismatch`. This preserves the locked gsplat/CUDA identity, the frozen fixture and camera, the existing tolerance, and the render/Evidence Policy contract: it neither truncates contributor support nor substitutes attribution, and it changes no per-Gaussian evidence beyond the flipped boundary mass itself. The alternative of avoiding the boundary through a fixture revision was rejected because the defect is a property of the locked gsplat build, not of one scene; real captures can carry boundary Gaussians at any pixel, and silently depending on lucky fixtures would leave the production path unable to explain its own failures.
