# Reconcile gsplat validity-boundary contributor flips against the RGB rasterization

The locked gsplat revision evaluates the shared per-Gaussian alpha in two separate CUDA translation units: the RGB rasterization kernel and the complete contributor-ID kernels. Their compiled float32 arithmetic disagrees by a few ulps, which is immaterial except exactly at the kernels' shared decision boundaries — the `1/255` validity cut, the `1e-4` transmittance termination cut, and sigma zero — where one kernel can accept a contributor the other rejected. Measured on the frozen controlled-overlap fixture at the locked 1008-pixel Anchor, exactly one of 1,016,064 pixels differed: the contributor stream accepted a Gaussian whose exact alpha is `0.003921566848368366`, `1.78e-9` below the validity cut, while the RGB kernel excluded it, producing a `0.0012227` mass mismatch (`rendererMassMismatch`) that blocked the production trial. The contributor kernels, not the RGB kernel, made the boundary error; the float64 reference chain reproduces the RGB alpha to `3.3e-8`.

The production renderer will reconcile only proven boundary flips and fail closed on anything else. When a pixel's contributor mass exceeds the unchanged `2e-6 + 1e-5 * abs(alpha)` tolerance, the backend replays that pixel's tile from the same projection/tile preparation in deterministic float64 with exact float32 rounding, enumerates at most four contributors whose decisions sit within float32 evaluation noise of a boundary, and keeps the unique decision variant whose recomputed alpha matches the RGB rasterization's own alpha within `1e-6`, far inside the tolerance the flipped weight must exceed to fail. The pixel's contributor IDs and weights are then rebuilt from the matched chain, so attribution stays tied to the same rasterization that produced service RGB and alpha. Any mismatch no boundary variant explains — a missing or extra non-boundary contributor, a divergent raster alpha, more than four candidates, or multiple matching variants — still raises `rendererMassMismatch`. This preserves the locked gsplat/CUDA identity, the frozen fixture and camera, the existing tolerance, and the render/Evidence Policy contract: it neither truncates contributor support nor substitutes attribution, and it changes no per-Gaussian evidence beyond the flipped boundary mass itself. The alternative of avoiding the boundary through a fixture revision was rejected because the defect is a property of the locked gsplat build, not of one scene; real captures can carry boundary Gaussians at any pixel, and silently depending on lucky fixtures would leave the production path unable to explain its own failures.
